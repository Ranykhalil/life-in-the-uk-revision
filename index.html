The UK & its Geography

What the UK actually is trips more people up than any history date. Lock in the UK / Great Britain / dependencies distinction first.

What is the UK?

  • The UK = England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland
  • Great Britain = England, Scotland and Wales only — NI is not part of GB
  • Location: north-west of Europe
  • Crown dependencies (own governments, not part of UK): Channel Islands & Isle of Man
  • British overseas territories (linked, not part of UK): St Helena & the Falkland Islands
CUEGB = the big island only. Close to home = Crown dependencies; far away = overseas territories. Neither is “part of the UK”.

Capitals

  • England: London  ·  Scotland: Edinburgh
  • Wales: Cardiff  ·  N. Ireland: Belfast
  • Swansea and Snowdonia are in Wales

Money

  • Currency: pound sterling (£)
  • Not a real coin: 25p · not a real note: £25
  • “£100 is the highest note” — False (it’s £50)
  • Scotland & NI print their own banknotes — valid everywhere in the UK
CUENo quarter: the UK has no 25-anything — no 25p, no £25.

National parks & nature

  • National parks in England, Wales & Scotland: 15 — areas of protected countryside
  • England’s largest national park: the Lake District — its Wastwater voted Britain’s favourite view (2007)
  • Loch Lomond & the Trossachs: Scotland · Snowdonia: Wales
  • Charity preserving buildings, coastline, countryside: the National Trust
  • Environmental charity: Friends of the Earth

Landmarks

  • Big Ben — the bell at the Houses of Parliament
  • Tower of London — built by William the Conqueror (the White Tower); holds the Crown Jewels; guides are Beefeaters (Yeoman Warders)
  • Cenotaph — a war memorial in Whitehall, designed by Sir Edwin Lutyens
  • Stonehenge: Wiltshire · Eden Project: Cornwall
  • Giant’s Causeway (volcanic columns, ~50m yrs old): Northern Ireland
  • Skara Brae (best-preserved prehistoric village): Orkney, Scotland
  • Crathes Castle: Scotland · Theatreland: London’s West End · Tate galleries: London
CUEOne landmark per nation: Causeway = NI, Skara Brae = Scotland, Snowdonia = Wales, Stonehenge/Eden/Lakes = England.

People of the UK

  • Share of population with a parent or grandparent born abroad: about 10%
  • Women today make up about half the workforce
  • Most shops open seven days a week (shorter hours Sundays/holidays)

Values & Your Responsibilities

Fundamental principles of British life

  • Democracy
  • The rule of law
  • Individual liberty
  • Tolerance of different faiths and beliefs
  • Participation in community life
CUED-R-I-T-P: “Democracy Runs on Individuals, Tolerance and Participation.” Monarchy and “joining a party” are not principles — common wrong options.

Your responsibilities as a citizen / resident

  • Respect and obey the law
  • Respect the rights of others, including their opinions
  • Treat others with fairness
  • Look after yourself and your family
  • Look after your area and the environment
CUETwo “respects”, two “look afters”, one “fairness”. “Stay in the UK forever” and “promote work” are decoys.

Getting set up

  • National Insurance number — phone the Department for Work and Pensions (DWP)
  • Documents needed: proof of identity + proof of permission to work
  • National Citizen Service — for 16–17 year olds, and it is optional, not compulsory
  • Suspected extremism/terrorism — contact your local police force
  • School governors’ key roles: set the strategic direction, ensure accountability, monitor and evaluate school performance

History — the Central Line

Early Britain
Stone Age First people were hunter-gatherers
6,000 yrs First farmers arrive, ancestors from south-east Europe
Iron Age First coins minted in Britain, some inscribed with names of Iron Age kings
55 BC Julius Caesar leads the first (failed) Roman invasion
AD 43 Emperor Claudius invades successfully; Boudicca, queen of the Iceni, fights the Romans
c. 122 Emperor Hadrian builds a wall to keep out the Picts — forts Housesteads & Vindolanda
AD 410 Romans leave; Jutes, Angles and Saxons invade from northern Europe
~600s St Augustine spreads Christianity — first Archbishop of Canterbury
800s Anglo-Saxon kingdoms unite under Alfred the Great, who defeats the Vikings
CUEInvader order = C-C-A-S-V-N: Caesar, Claudius, Anglo-Saxons, Vikings, Normans. Caesar failed, Claudius nailed it.
Middle Ages
1066 Norman Conquest — last successful invasion. William the Conqueror (Duke of Normandy, France) beats Harold at Hastings; recorded in the Bayeux Tapestry
after 1066 Domesday Book — William’s survey of towns, people and animals. Nobles speak Norman French, peasants Anglo-Saxon → the two merge into English
1215 Magna Carta — even the king is subject to the law; restricted his power to collect taxes and change laws
1314 Robert the Bruce beats the English at Bannockburn — Scotland stays unconquered
1348 Black Death (a plague) kills one third of England (similar in Scotland & Wales)
1400s Hundred Years War with France (actually 116 years); serfs = peasants farming a small patch of their lord’s land
1455 Wars of the Roses: House of Lancaster vs House of York
1400s Chaucer writes The Canterbury Tales — poems about a pilgrimage; William Caxton first to print books in England
CUEDates that spell themselves: Magna Carta = 12:15 on the clock. Bannockburn = 13-14 counting up. Black Death = 1348 (“fourth-eighth of the people gone — worse: a third”).
Tudors & Stuarts
1530s Henry VIII founds the Church of England because the Pope refused his divorce from Catherine of Aragon; the movement against the Pope’s authority = the Reformation
1536 Anne Boleyn executed at the Tower of London
1560 Scotland’s Parliament establishes a Protestant Church of Scotland (Presbyterian, elected leadership, not a state church)
1558–1603 Elizabeth I — daughter of Henry VIII, a Protestant who balanced Catholic and Protestant views; under her, settlers begin to colonise America’s east coast
1588 English defeat the Spanish Armada — sent by Spain to restore Catholicism
1587 Mary, Queen of Scots — a Catholic, Elizabeth’s cousin — executed after 20 years in prison, accused of plotting
1603 James VI of Scotland becomes James I of England; his English Bible = the King James / Authorised Version
1642 Civil War: king’s Cavaliers vs Parliament’s Roundheads. Charles I believed in the Divine Right of Kings and tried to rule without Parliament
1645 Charles I defeated at Marston Moor and Naseby
1649 Charles I executed → England becomes a republic (“the Commonwealth”); Oliver Cromwell = Lord Protector (until 1658); Puritans were strict Protestants
1660 Charles II restored from exile in the Netherlands — had escaped by hiding in an oak tree
1666 Great Fire of London destroys St Paul’s — rebuilt by Sir Christopher Wren
1679 Habeas Corpus Act — every prisoner has a right to a court hearing
1688 Glorious Revolution — Protestants invite William of Orange to take the throne, unresisted
1689 Bill of Rights — confirms the rights of Parliament and limits of the king’s power (NOT votes!) → start of constitutional monarchy
1690 William defeats James II at the Battle of the Boyne (Ireland)
1680–1720 Huguenot refugees arrive from France
CUECharles I lost his head; Charles II hid in a tree. Fire of London: 1666 — “the devil’s number burned the city.” Stuart drumbeat: 42 war · 49 axe · 60 return · 66 fire · 79 habeas · 88 glorious · 89 bill · 90 Boyne.
Georgians & the Empire
1721–42 Sir Robert Walpole — the first Prime Minister
1745 Bonnie Prince Charlie (Charles Edward Stuart) raises an army of Highland clansmen; earlier the MacDonalds of Glencoe were killed for not taking the oath; landlords later destroy crofts in the Highland Clearances
1700s The Enlightenment — new ideas in politics, philosophy, science: Adam Smith → economics, David Hume → philosophy
1700s Industrial Revolution — driven by steam power; manufacturing becomes the biggest employer; canals built to link factories to towns and ports; Britain in conflict with France over trade; captain James Cook maps Australia
1776 13 North American colonies declare independence over taxation (“no taxation without representation”)
1805 Battle of Trafalgar — Admiral Nelson commands the fleet and is killed
1815 Battle of Waterloo — the Duke of Wellington defeats Napoleon; the last battle between Britain and France
CUENelson at sea ’05, Wellington on land ’15 — the sailor comes first, and only the soldier survives.
Victorians
1833 Emancipation Act abolishes slavery across the Empire; William Wilberforce — a politician (MP) and abolitionist
1846 Corn Laws repealed — free trade by abolishing import taxes
1847 Factories Act — women & children limited to 10 hours a day
1851 Great Exhibition in Hyde Park, inside the Crystal Palace
1853–56 Crimean War — Britain + Turkey + France vs Russia; first war extensively covered by the media; Florence Nightingale — a nurse, founder of modern nursing
1840s Irish potato famine — a million die; the Fenians want complete Irish independence
1800s Isambard Kingdom Brunel builds the Great Western Railway (first major railway, from Paddington)
1899–1902 Boer War in South Africa — the Boers were settlers from the Netherlands
CUEVictorian reforms go 33 → 46 → 47 → 51: free the people, free the trade, free the workers, show it off (Emancipation, Corn Laws, Factories Act, Great Exhibition).
20th century
1913–21 Home Rule promised for Ireland, postponed by WWI; Ireland becomes a republic in 1949
1914 Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand triggers WWI — which ends at 11am, 11 November 1918
1918 Women over 30 get the vote — recognising their WWI war effort. Campaign led by the suffragettes under Emmeline Pankhurst
1928 Women vote at 21, same as men
1930s Great Depression hits heavy industry — especially shipbuilding
1939 Germany invades Poland → WWII. Winston Churchill becomes wartime PM (voted greatest Briton, 2002)
1940 Dunkirk: 300,000+ men rescued by small boats. Battle of Britain: an aerial battle won by the RAF
1944 Butler Act — free secondary education (England & Wales)
1945 Labour wins; Clement Attlee PM; the United Nations set up after WWII
1969 Voting age lowered to 18
1979–90 Margaret Thatcher — first woman PM and longest-serving of the 20th century; succeeded by John Major (NI peace process)
1997 Tony Blair (Labour) — introduces the Scottish Parliament and Welsh Assembly
2010 Coalition: Conservatives + Liberal Democrats, David Cameron PM
CUEVotes for women: years up, ages down — 1918/age 30 → 1928/age 21 → 1969/age 18.
Inventors & scientists
1687 Isaac Newton — gravity (Principia Mathematica)
1920s John Logie Baird (Scot) — television
1930s Frank Whittle — jet engine · Robert Watson-Watt — radar · Alan Turing — the Turing machine
1930s Ernest Rutherford — first to split the atom (Manchester → Cambridge)

Government & Democracy

The system

  • The UK is a parliamentary democracy with a constitutional monarchy
  • Written constitution? No — it is unwritten (no single document)
  • Not a constitutional institution: the armed forces
  • Parliament = House of Commons + House of Lords

The monarch

  • Appoints the government chosen at election; advises, warns and encourages the PM (who makes the actual decisions)
  • Head of the Church of England (spiritual leader = Archbishop of Canterbury)
  • Appoints life peers on the advice of the Prime Minister

Commons, Lords & the Speaker

  • Debates in the Commons chaired by the Speaker — who is an MP with a constituency
  • Lords (“peers”) are not elected; several Church of England bishops sit there
  • MPs’ jobs: represent their constituency, help create new laws, scrutinise government, debate national issues
  • Contact an MP by letter, phone, constituency office or local surgeries — not post-only
  • Official reports of Parliament: Hansard; PM’s Questions happen every week
  • Opposition’s senior team = the shadow cabinet — challenges government, offers alternatives

Elections & voting

  • General elections at least every 5 years; MP dies/resigns → by-election
  • Voting age 18 (set in 1969); before voting you’re sent a poll card
  • Stand as MP / public office at 18 (exceptions: armed forces, civil servants, some convicts)
  • Persuading people to vote for a party = canvassing
  • TV & radio election coverage must be balanced — equal time to rival views (by law)
  • ‘Individual registration’ is used in Northern Ireland (not Scotland/Wales)

PM, ministers & civil service

  • PM’s official home: 10 Downing Street
  • Home Secretary runs: crime, policing and immigration
  • Civil servants are chosen on merit and politically neutral — not appointed by government
  • Towns and rural areas are run by democratically elected councils (“local authorities”) — not appointed officials

Devolution

  • Wales — the Senedd: 60 members, elected every 4 years by proportional representation
  • Northern Ireland Assembly: 90 MLAs, elected by PR; it has been suspended in the past
  • Scottish Parliament can legislate on education, health, civil/criminal law, planning, some taxes
  • Devolved everywhere: health & education
  • Stays with Westminster: defence, foreign affairs, immigration, taxation, social security
CUENorthern Ireland = Ninety; Senedd = Sixty. Westminster keeps anything with an army, a border or a wallet.

Beyond the UK

  • The Commonwealth: an association of countries working towards democracy and development — mostly ex-Empire
  • The UN was set up after the Second World War to promote peace
  • Is the BBC government-controlled? No — state-funded but independent

Law, Courts & Everyday Rules

Juries

  • Jury size in England, Wales & NI: 12 — in Scotland: 15
  • Chosen randomly from the electoral register, ages 18–70
  • Want jury duty? Be on the electoral register
CUEScotland does it bigger: 12 → 15. Same pattern as small claims: England £10k, Scotland/NI £5k.

Courts

  • Minor crime in England/Wales/NI: Magistrates’ Court · in Scotland: Justice of the Peace Court
  • Youth Court — ages 10–17, heard by up to 3 trained magistrates or a District Judge; public excluded, names/photos cannot be published; the most serious cases go to Crown Court
  • Small claims — an informal way to settle minor disputes without a lawyer: under £10,000 (England & Wales), £5,000 (Scotland & NI)
  • Solicitors charge mostly by time spent on the case
CUE“Scotland keeps the Peace” — Justice of the Peace Court.

Criminal vs civil

  • Criminal offences: racial crime, smoking where banned, selling alcohol to under-18s, FGM
  • Civil law example: discrimination in the workplace
  • Violence towards a partner: can be prosecuted — and a husband forcing his wife to have sex is rape
  • Forced marriage — court orders can protect a person

Police complaints

  • Anyone can complain by going to a police station or writing to the Chief Constable
  • Independent bodies: IPCC (England & Wales), Police Complaints Commissioner (Scotland)
  • “Only by writing to X” statements are False — there are always several routes

Age limits

  • 16 — ride a moped; NCS programme; drink with a meal in a hotel/restaurant
  • 17 — drive a car or motorcycle (licence required)
  • 18 — vote; stand as MP; buy alcohol; betting shops & gambling; jury service (to 70)
CUEEscalator: 16 moped → 17 motor car → 18 (almost) everything else.

Everyday rules

  • Dogs in public must wear a collar showing the owner’s name and address
  • To drive on public roads you need a valid driving licence

Culture, Sport & Traditions

Religious calendar

  • Christmas Day, 25 Dec — birth of Jesus; Boxing Day = 26 Dec
  • Easter (in March or April): Good Friday = Jesus’s death, Easter Sunday = rising
  • The 40 days before Easter: Lent
  • Diwali — celebrated by Hindus and Sikhs; called the Festival of Lights
  • Eid al-Fitr — end of Ramadan, after a month of fasting
  • Head of the Church of England: the monarch; Church of Scotland is Presbyterian; Wales & NI have no established church

Secular days

  • Halloween: 31 October · April Fool’s: 1 April, jokes until midday
  • Scottish New Year’s Eve (31 Dec): Hogmanay; New Year song = Auld Lang Syne
  • Public holidays are called bank holidays — banks and businesses close
  • Remembrance Day service is held at the Cenotaph

Sport

  • Golf — 15th-century Scotland · tennis — Wimbledon
  • Cricket vs Australia: the Ashes · rugby: Six Nations
  • Horse racing: Grand National (Aintree), Royal Ascot (Berkshire, 5 days, royals attend), Scottish Grand National (Ayr); museum at Newmarket, Suffolk
  • Thames rowing race: Oxford vs Cambridge
  • UK hosted the Olympics 3 times: 1908, 1948, 2012

Sporting heroes

  • 1966 World Cup-winning captain: Bobby Moore
  • First 4-minute mile (1954): Roger Bannister
  • 1972 Olympic pentathlon gold: Mary Peters
  • Famous Paralympians: Tanni Grey-Thompson & Ellie Simmonds (Simmonds: swimming golds 2008, 2012, 2016)

Writers & artists

  • Shakespeare — born in Stratford-upon-Avon; wrote A Midsummer Night’s Dream, Macbeth
  • ‘The Daffodils’, inspired by nature: William Wordsworth
  • Sherlock Holmes: Sir Arthur Conan Doyle
  • Best-loved novel 2003: The Lord of the Rings — JRR Tolkien
  • Pop art (1960s): David Hockney · Art Deco ceramics: Clarice Cliff
  • ‘Pomp and Circumstance’: Sir Edward Elgar (a musician)

Stage, screen & prizes

  • Silent-film tramp: Charlie Chaplin
  • Wallace & Gromit’s creator (4 Oscars, animation): Nick Park
  • Highest-grossing UK film franchises: Harry Potter & James Bond
  • Actress who has NOT won an Oscar: Emily Watson
  • Man Booker Prize: fiction/literature · Turner Prize: contemporary art
  • The Proms: 8-week summer classical music season (Royal Albert Hall)
  • “The Fringe” festival: Edinburgh
CUEPrize pairing: Booker = books; Turner = (modern) art — think Turner the painter.

Mind the gap — true/false traps

These are the statements the exams love to twist. Learn the correction, not just the verdict.

False — and why

  • “The Bill of Rights (1689) gave votes to men / women” — False: it confirmed Parliament’s rights and limited the king
  • “Northern Ireland is part of Great Britain” — False: GB = England, Scotland, Wales
  • “St Helena / Falklands / Channel Islands are part of the UK” — False: linked to the UK, not part of it
  • “Wales / NI has its own established church” — False: neither has one
  • “Peers in the Lords are elected” — False: they are appointed / inherit
  • “Councils are government-appointed officials” — False: democratically elected
  • “You must be 21 to vote / stand as MP / sit on a jury” — False: 18 for all three
  • “The NI Assembly has never been suspended” — False: it has, several times
  • “Young offenders’ names can be published” — False: Youth Court identities are protected
  • “Scotland & Wales use individual registration” — False: that’s Northern Ireland

True — but sounds wrong

  • Scottish & NI banknotes are valid everywhere in the UK — True
  • Church of England bishops sit in the House of Lords — True
  • The Speaker is a regular MP with constituents — True
  • A husband who forces his wife to have sex commits rape — True
  • You can complain about police straight to the Chief Constable — True (and other routes exist)
  • The BBC is state-funded yet independent of government — True

Easily-confused pairs

  • Trafalgar 1805 (Nelson, sea, dies) vs Waterloo 1815 (Wellington, land, wins)
  • 1918 = women 30+ vs 1928 = equal at 21
  • Magistrates’ Court (E/W/NI) vs Justice of the Peace Court (Scotland)
  • Crown dependencies = Channel Is. + Isle of Man vs overseas territories = St Helena + Falklands
  • Cavaliers = King vs Roundheads = Parliament
  • Charles I — executed 1649 vs Charles II — oak tree, restored 1660
  • Domesday Book = survey vs Bayeux Tapestry = battle embroidery (both tell of William I’s England)
  • Reformation = against the Pope (Henry VIII) vs Enlightenment = new ideas, 18th century
